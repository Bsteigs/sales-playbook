Open — the upfront contract (first 60 seconds)
Say this before any question "Thanks for the time today. As a loose agenda, I'd love to spend most of this understanding your data environment, the AI initiatives you're building on top of it, and where things break down or need to improve. I can infer from public information, but I don't want to assume. If we feel we can help, we can talk about what that looks like and next steps if there's alignment. Sound good?"
  • Signals you won't pitch immediately, and gives them permission to say it's not a fit — which makes them engage more.
Situation — understand the environment
Situation "Walk me through your current data infrastructure at a high level — primary warehouses, transformation layer, what you're using for orchestration?"
Listening for Snowflake, dbt, Airflow. If they name them, you have instant credibility and integration footing.
Situation · the pivot question "Do you have monitoring or observability in place today specifically for data quality — as distinct from infrastructure monitoring?"
Almost everyone says Datadog or Grafana — which watches the application layer, not the data layer. That's the gap. If they say "not really," the door is open.
Problem — surface where it breaks
Problem · the most important question "When a data pipeline breaks or produces incorrect data — how does your team typically find out?"
The answer is almost always "the business tells us." That's BAU in one sentence. Let them say it. Don't finish it for them. Then label it: "So the business becomes the alert system."
Problem · the debugging burden "Walk me through what the investigation looks like — from 'something's wrong' to 'we know what broke and why.' What does that process actually look like?"
Surfaces the manual archaeology, the on-call stress. Quantifies the operational cost of BAU.
Problem · get the metric "How much of your data engineering team's week goes toward debugging and firefighting versus building new capabilities?"
Industry average is ~40%. When they confirm a number, they've quantified BAU in their own words. That's your business-case input.
Problem · the dbt-coverage gap "Do you find there are scenarios your dbt tests weren't scripted to catch that the team finds out about after the fact? Particularly distribution — where the values were there, the row count was right, but a number was off in a way nobody wrote a rule for. Like volatility showing 0.02% instead of 15% because a decimal dropped."
Specific example makes the gap concrete. Separates rules-you-defined from anomalies-you-didn't-anticipate.
Implication — connect to business + regulatory impact
Implication "When data problems reach the business before your team catches them — what's the downstream impact? Analyst productivity, wrong decisions, or something with regulatory weight?"
Shifts from operational pain to business consequence. The CDO's ears perk up here.
Implication · regulated-firm specific "What's the exposure if a data quality issue propagates into a NAV calculation, a risk model output, or a regulatory submission before it's caught?"
Names their actual consequences without making it an attack. The CDO usually takes this one.
Implication · agent trust pivot "You mentioned AI systems supporting your teams. How confident are you in the quality of the data those systems pull from — is that a known risk or an assumed risk?"
"Assumed risk" vs "known risk" is the power distinction. Most admit it's assumed. Opens the agent-trust conversation — MC's newest, most defensible territory.
Financial services & regulated industry — specific angles
  • These questions require genuine FS buyer knowledge. Use them after you've established the operational pain — they elevate the conversation to board and regulatory stakes no generic observability vendor can credibly navigate.
Regulatory · SEC AI governance "Given where the SEC is going on AI governance — if an examiner asked you today to produce the data lineage behind a specific AI output from last quarter, what would that process look like for your team? Is that a 10-minute answer or a 2-week investigation?"
Forces the CDO to confront auditability in concrete regulatory terms. "Examiner asked today" makes it real, not hypothetical. Almost no firm has a 10-minute answer — and when they admit that, you've surfaced the gap without characterizing it yourself.
Regulatory · LP due diligence "When your next institutional allocator runs their data governance DDQ — and they get to the question about how you systematically monitor and document data quality in your AI systems — what's your team's current answer? A documented, defensible response, or more of a 'we do our best' conversation?"
LP DDQ is a capital-gating event — a failed DDQ can block $50M-$500M in uncommitted capital. "Documented and defensible vs. best efforts" is the specific contrast that lands. CDOs know exactly which camp they're in, and the uncomfortable ones will tell you.
Regulatory · AI audit trail "You mentioned your AI research assistant. Walk me through what happens if a portfolio manager makes a decision based on its output — and two days later you find out the underlying data was stale by 18 hours at the time. Who finds out? What's the conversation with that PM? And what documentation exists to explain what happened?"
Three sub-questions force them to trace through the actual process — detection, communication, and documentation — and confront the absence of each. Most teams have no answer to the third question. That silence is the moment.
Guided business implication questions — specific scenarios, not open-ended impact asks
  • "What's the business impact?" is an unanswerable question. Ask about a specific scenario — the last real incident, a concrete failure mode — so the buyer can actually respond and quantify the cost themselves.
Guided implication · real incident anchor "When a data problem reaches a PM or analyst before your team catches it — not every incident, just the last notable one — what happened? Who called who? How long before you knew what actually broke?"
"Not every incident, just the last notable one" removes the ability to answer abstractly. Anchoring in a real event they've lived through surfaces authentic pain, gives you a story to reference throughout the cycle, and establishes BAU more powerfully than any hypothetical.
Guided implication · detection scenario "If a Snowflake table that powers your daily risk report hadn't refreshed since 3am — and it's now 9am — and your team didn't know: how would you find out? Who would call who? How long until someone notices?"
Specific, realistic scenario. Forces them to walk through their actual detection process out loud. If the answer is "one of the analysts would probably flag it in Slack" — that's BAU confirmed in their own words without you naming it.
Guided implication · blast radius "When something breaks upstream and cascades into multiple dashboards simultaneously — how long before you know the full blast radius? And how do you notify the business teams affected when you don't yet know which ones to call first?"
Tests whether they experience lineage as a pain point. "When you don't yet know which ones to call first" pins the specific gap — almost nobody has an answer, and when they don't, lineage is the direct solution you introduce.
Guided implication · opportunity cost "You mentioned your team spends roughly [X]% of the week firefighting. If you could redirect that time to building — what's the one initiative that would ship first? And what's that initiative worth to the business if it ships 6 months sooner?"
Turns a time metric into a business value metric. Gets them to articulate the opportunity cost in their own words. Whatever they say becomes your ROI anchor for the business case and the close conversation.
Compute cost & CI/CD — the gaps most discovery calls miss
Compute cost · invisible waste "When a pipeline failure isn't caught immediately and Airflow keeps running jobs on corrupted or incomplete data for several hours — do you have visibility into how much Snowflake compute those unnecessary runs consumed? Has anyone calculated that number?"
Most teams haven't calculated this. If they haven't, you've surfaced a cost that was invisible — and MC's circuit breakers (which automatically stop Airflow DAGs the moment an error is detected) become an ROI story beyond data quality alone. The waste from one bad overnight batch run can cost thousands in Snowflake credits.
Compute cost · unused assets "Do you have visibility into what percentage of your Snowflake tables, fields, and queries are actively used versus sitting unused? And who's responsible for identifying what can be deprecated to reduce your compute footprint?"
Storage and compute costs from unused assets are a common, invisible Snowflake pain. MC surfaces which tables, fields, and queries are unused — enabling deprecation and bill reduction. For a CFO or COO this is a direct ROI conversation that bypasses the "data quality" budget category entirely.
CI/CD · monitoring at build time "Right now, when a data engineer pushes a change to a dbt model or a transformation — is there any data quality gate before it hits production? Or does the first signal that something's wrong come from downstream, after it's already caused an issue?"
Tests whether monitoring is embedded in their build process. Almost every team says "downstream." That's the CI/CD gap — MC can embed quality checks into the code review stage so bad changes don't reach production in the first place. This is the reactive-to-preventive shift the Claude integration represents.
Need-payoff — make them describe the future
Need-payoff "If your team had automated visibility into data issues before the business ever felt them — what would that change operationally?"
Never describe the future for them. When the data engineer says "we could build instead of firefight" — that's their PBO in their words.
Need-payoff · AI layer "What would it mean to have a layer that could trace a bad AI output back to the exact data pipeline that caused it?"
When the CDO says "I could give the board a defensible answer" — that's their PBO. Reference these exact phrases for the rest of the cycle.
The insight — deliver after implication lands
Challenger reframe — then go quiet "What we see consistently at your scale is that the gap isn't about tools or coverage — it's about the layer. Job-level monitoring tells you whether your pipelines ran. It tells you nothing about whether the output is trustworthy. And that's where the PM ends up being the alert system."

Then: "Does that resonate with how you're experiencing it?"

The POC — agree on success criteria before you start
+

The single most important POC question
Establish before any eval begins "What would need to be true at the end of a 30-day evaluation for you to feel confident this is worth the investment?"

Whatever they say becomes the success criteria. Build the POC to prove exactly that.
The POC motion — what it actually looks like
  • Week 1: Connect to Snowflake. MC reads metadata, builds baselines. No data leaves the environment, no sensitive data accessed.
  • Week 2: First anomaly detections surface — stale tables, volume deviations, schema changes the team didn't know about or was hearing about from the business.
  • Weeks 2–4: Lineage maps build out. Team sees the full dependency picture and downstream blast radius.
  • End of POC: Point to specific incidents MC caught before the business felt them. That's the proof — real issues in their real environment, not a demo.
Why the proof is structurally strong
  • The proof isn't a slide — it's MC finding problems they didn't know they had. That's almost impossible to argue with.
  • Get the champion to co-own the success criteria so the win is theirs to present internally.

Multi-thread
Map the committee, secure EB access, keep everyone looped in early
+
MEDDPICC: E / Champion CoM: keep BAU + metrics alive
The principle
  • Champion ≠ economic buyer. At enterprise scale the data engineer or VP Data is your champion; the CDO (or CTO above them) signs. Know which is which.
  • Champion test: can they sell when you're not in the room? Will they set up the EB call? If not, they're a coach.
  • Map every stakeholder in the first two meetings. For each: what's their pain, their decision criteria, what would make them say no.
Securing EB access — the calibrated ask
Not a demand — a partnership requirement "To make sure what I'm building actually addresses your leadership's priorities, I need to understand them directly. What does that path look like?"
Pitfall #1 — Blocked from the EB at the final stage
  • The trap: You run the whole cycle with the champion, then at decision time you've never met the person who signs. Resources extracted, EB dark, deal stalls or dies. (The Pantera / CardWorks lesson.)
  • The fix — loop the EB in early. From the first POC update, include the EB on a brief weekly/bi-weekly status email. Even if they never reply, they're absorbing progress, pain, and metrics. By decision time you're a known quantity, not a stranger.
  • "Act as if." Operate from the first meeting as though the EB is already part of the deal — reference their priorities, address their concerns in your updates, make the champion comfortable forwarding upward.
  • Why it works: Free extraction trains champions that asking costs nothing. Establishing give-and-take early — and keeping the EB visible — changes the dynamic from vendor evaluation to partnership.
What a good status update contains
  • Pain — the problem in their own words, restated.
  • Progress — what the POC / eval has surfaced so far.
  • Criteria — the success criteria they defined, and status against each.
  • Metrics — quantified findings (issues caught, time saved, coverage gained).
  • Tied to business objectives — connect every update back to the initiative and outcome leadership actually cares about, not the tooling.
Pitfall #2 — Single-threaded on the champion
  • The trap: One relationship, one point of failure. Champion changes roles, goes quiet, or loses influence — deal collapses.
  • The fix: Use the champion to make introductions. A real champion opens doors; a coach can't. Their willingness to multi-thread you is itself the champion test.
Pitfall #3 — Competitor surfaces late
  • The trap: A rival shows up in the final third and you're caught flat. (The Vertex MSSP, the platform-native pitch.)
  • The fix: Inoculate early. "If a platform-native option or an internal-build proposal surfaces later, what would concern you most?" Plant the frame before the competitor arrives.

The reframes — pick the one that fits the room

Three non-obvious insights to reframe the conversation. Each lands differently depending on who's across the table. Deliver after pain surfaces — never as an opener.

Reframe 01 — the executive / strategic room
If everyone has the same frontier models, the differentiation isn't the model. It's your data — and whether you can trust it.
When every firm can access the same frontier AI, the model stops being the advantage. The edge becomes the proprietary data you hold and the insights you derive from it. Which means data reliability stops being a back-office function and becomes a source of competitive advantage. The firms that can trust, learn from, and act on their own data drive outsized outcomes. The ones that can't deploy the same models as everyone else — on a foundation they can't verify.
Reframe 02 — the data engineer / technical room
The gap isn't a tooling problem. It's a layer problem.
Job-level monitoring tells you whether your pipelines ran. dbt tests catch the failures you scripted in advance. Neither tells you whether the output is actually trustworthy — whether a distribution shifted, a value drifted, or a schema changed in a way nobody wrote a rule for. That's the layer where the business ends up being your alert system. You're not under-tooled. You're monitoring the wrong altitude.
Reframe 03 — the AI / agent room
Your AI agents don't fail loudly. They fail confidently.
A broken application throws an error. A broken agent returns a well-formatted, confident, wrong answer built on stale or corrupted data — and the person acting on it has no signal anything is off. As you move agents into production, the risk isn't downtime. It's silent, plausible wrongness at scale, with no error code and no alarm. The only way to trust an output is to trust the data underneath it — and be able to trace the output back to its source.

Why AI is unreliable — the four root causes

Barr Moses' framework for why AI outputs fail. Monte Carlo sits at the data layer underneath all four — which is why it can trace a bad output back to its actual cause.

1
The data is wrong
Wrong values, didn't arrive, or didn't arrive on time. The input itself is bad — so the output is bad, no matter how good the model is.
2
The code is wrong
A change in transformation logic or agent code creates downstream issues. The pipeline runs; the logic silently produces something wrong.
3
The system fails
The infrastructure itself breaks. 100% of systems fail eventually — the question is whether you detect it before the business does.
4
The model output is off
Perfect prompt, perfect context — and it still hallucinates or isn't fit for purpose. The output needs monitoring even when everything upstream looks clean.

Before Datadog, engineers flew blind on application health — they found out services were down when users complained. Datadog built the layer that caught failures before users felt them. Monte Carlo does the same thing for data: it catches data failures before the business feels them. Same problem, different layer of the stack. Every technical buyer knows Datadog — so this makes a new category instantly understandable.

"The question every data team is answering today is: do you find out something's wrong before the business feels it, or after? Without observability, it's after. Monte Carlo makes it before — across data quality and the AI systems built on top of it."

Monte Carlo owns the data quality layer underneath the applications and models. When an AI agent produces a wrong answer, MC traces it back to the upstream pipeline that caused it — end-to-end lineage from source to dashboard to model output. No application-monitoring or model-monitoring tool has that. That's the defensible position.

The three platform agents — how the product actually works

Most buyers hear "MC" and think monitoring dashboard. The product is actually three specialized agents that handle detection, diagnosis, and operations. Understanding this distinction separates a credible conversation from a surface-level one — and it's how John and his team talk about the platform internally.

👁
Monitoring Agent
